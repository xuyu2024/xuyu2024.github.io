1.Transition-edge sensor (TES) detector

1.1 introduction

TES is a thermal equilibrium detector that uses superconductor film as a thermometer. In the transition region of a superconductor, a minute temperature variation can trigger a sharp change in resistance, thereby imparting a high energy resolution to the TES.(1.6eV@5.9keV)

The TES is biased in the superconducting transition region with a constant voltage. The incident energy is absorbed by the absorber and converted into heat within it. Due to the good thermal connection between the absorber and the superconducting film, almost all the heat flows into the superconducting film, causing an increase in its resistance. In the voltage-biased circuit, this increase in resistance leads to a decrease in current. Because the impedance of TES is very low, only SQUID amplifiers can be used at present to read out the changes in current, which is equivalent to obtaining the incident energy information. The heat flows into the heat bath through a structure that has a very weak thermal connection with the superconducting film. Then, the TES returns to its initial state, ready for the subsequent measurement.

2.Superconducting Tunnel Junction (STJ) detector

2.1 introduction

The superconducting tunnel junction (STJ) detector is a superconducting detector that utilizes the quasiparticle tunneling effect. Incident energy is absorbed by absorber in a superconducting state. The energy breaks Cooper pairs and an “excess” number of electronic excitations, called quasi-particles is created. The number of quasiparticles is positively correlated with the energy absorbed in the absorber. The incident energy can be measured by measuring the current generated during quasiparticle tunneling.

Since the energy gap of superconductors is typically in the meV range, while that of semiconductors is usually in the eV range, the same energy deposition in a superconductor generates approximately 1000 times more charge carriers than the number of electron-hole pairs generated in a semiconductor. This results in an approximately 30-fold improvement in the energy resolution of STJ detectors compared to semiconductor detectors (based on the approximation √1000). For example, in the case of niobium (Nb), the minimum energy required to generate quasiparticles is 2.5 meV, with a theoretical limit of energy resolution of 4 eV at 6 keV. If other superconductor materials with smaller energy gaps, such as tantalum (Ta) or hafnium (Hf), are used, even higher energy resolutions can be achieved.

Additionally, STJ detectors exhibit a higher counting rate (0.5 MHz) compared to other thermally responsive superconducting detectors, such as Transition-edge Sensors (TES), which typically have a counting rate of 2 kHz. As a result, STJ detectors are a superior choice for applications that require both high energy resolution and high counting rates.
